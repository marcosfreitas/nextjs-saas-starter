## Brand Naming Process

### Stage 1: Research & Discovery

**Business Context**:
- Business/product/service being named
- Business model (B2B, B2C, SaaS, etc.)
- Business stage and goals

**Target Audience**:
- Primary and secondary audiences
- Cultural context and language requirements
- Audience preferences

**Competitive Landscape**:
- Direct competitors (list 5-10)
- Naming patterns in category
- Differentiation opportunities
- Category clichés to avoid

**Brand Positioning**:
- Value proposition
- Brand personality and promise
- Emotional connection desired

**Naming Constraints**:
- Geographic scope
- Domain availability requirements
- Trademark considerations
- Budget for filing and acquisition

### Stage 2: Naming Strategy

**Naming Approaches** (can be mixed):
- **Descriptive**: Clearly describes what it does (PayPal, The Home Depot)
- **Suggestive**: Hints at benefits (Amazon, Slack, Uber)
- **Abstract/Invented**: Made-up words (Kodak, Spotify, Google)
- **Evocative**: Evokes emotions/experiences (Patagonia, Tesla, Apple)
- **Compound**: Combines two words (Facebook, Snapchat, Microsoft)
- **Disruptive**: Breaks category conventions (iFood, Nubank, Netflix)

**Hybrid Approach**: Mix styles creatively for memorability. Best names often combine multiple approaches.

**Naming Criteria**:
- Memorability, Pronounceability, Spellability
- Meaning, Distinctiveness, Scalability
- Ownability, Domain Availability

**Naming Style** (can be mixed):
- Professional/Corporate, Modern/Tech, Playful/Creative
- Luxury/Premium, Bold/Disruptive

### Stage 3: Name Generation

**Brainstorming Techniques**:
- Word association (concepts, benefits, emotions)
- Morphology (prefixes/suffixes, portmanteau)
- Foreign languages (culturally appropriate)
- Visual/phonetic play (alliteration, rhyme)

**Generation Process**:
- Generate 50-100+ candidates
- Don't self-edit during generation
- Mix styles and contexts
- Consider disruptive naming patterns
- Initial filtering for basic criteria

**Result**: 20-30 candidate names

### Stage 4: Evaluation & Refinement

Score each name (1-5 scale) on:
- Memorability
- Pronounceability
- Spellability
- Meaning/Relevance
- Distinctiveness
- Scalability
- Ownability
- Emotional Appeal

**Result**: 5-10 finalist names

### Stage 5: Validation & Testing

**Domain & Trademark Research**:
- Check .com, .io, .co domains
- Social handles availability
- Preliminary trademark search
- Google search for conflicts

**Audience Testing** (if budget allows):
- Focus groups or interviews
- Surveys with target audience

**Internal Validation**:
- Stakeholder review
- Check for negative associations

### Stage 6: Selection & Implementation

**Final Selection**:
- Highest overall score
- Best balance of criteria
- Strongest domain/trademark position
- Best audience reception

**Implementation Plan**:
- File trademark application
- Register domains
- Secure social handles
- Develop visual identity
- Create brand guidelines

## Brand Strategy Process

### Stage 1: Research & Analysis

- Business analysis (model, goals, position)
- Audience research (demographics, psychographics, journey)
- Competitive analysis (positioning, messaging, gaps)
- Brand audit (if existing brand)

### Stage 2: Positioning Strategy

**Positioning Statement**:
For [target audience] who [need], [Brand] is [category] that [benefit]. Unlike [competitor], we [proof].

**Brand Positioning**:
- Price, Quality, Innovation positioning
- Emotional positioning

**Differentiation Strategy**:
- What makes us different
- What we can own
- What customers value most

### Stage 3: Brand Definition

- Brand Purpose: Why we exist
- Brand Vision: Where we're going
- Brand Mission: What we do
- Brand Values: What we stand for
- Brand Personality: How we behave
- Brand Voice & Tone: How we communicate

### Stage 4: Brand Expression

- Visual Identity (logo, colors, typography, imagery)
- Messaging Framework (core messages, value prop, tagline)
- Brand Guidelines (standards, usage, examples)

### Stage 5: Execution & Evolution

- Brand Launch (strategy, timeline, education)
- Brand Management (monitoring, audits, metrics)

## Best Practices

### Naming Best Practices

1. Keep it simple (short, easy to say/spell)
2. Make it memorable (distinctive, unique)
3. Mix styles & contexts creatively
4. Consider disruptive naming for innovative products
5. Ensure meaning (convey something relevant)
6. Check availability (domain, trademark, social)
7. Test with audience
8. Think long-term (scalability)
9. Consider global implications
10. Protect it (trademark, domains)

### Brand Strategy Best Practices

1. Start with research (market, audience, competitors)
2. Define clear positioning
3. Be consistent across touchpoints
4. Evolve strategically
5. Measure performance
6. Protect brand assets
7. Build over time (long-term investment)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Naming Mistakes**:
- Too clever/obscure
- Too generic (not ownable)
- Too long/complex
- Ignoring domain availability
- Not checking trademarks
- Not testing with audience

**Brand Strategy Mistakes**:
- No clear positioning
- Trying to be everything to everyone
- Inconsistent brand expression
- Ignoring competitor positioning
- Not aligning with business strategy
- Changing brand too frequently

## Disruptive Naming Patterns

For innovative/disruptive products, consider:

**Techniques**:
- Prefix Disruption: i-, nu-, neo-, meta-, super-
- Suffix Innovation: -ify, -ly, -io, -ia, -ex
- Portmanteau Creation: Blend words naturally
- Category Breaking: Use conventions from different industry
- Minimalist Disruption: Ultra-short names
- Cultural Fusion: Blend words from different languages

**Examples**:
- iFood: Tech prefix disrupts food delivery
- Spotify: Portmanteau disrupts music streaming
- Nubank: "New" prefix disrupts banking
- Uber: Evocative word disrupts transportation
